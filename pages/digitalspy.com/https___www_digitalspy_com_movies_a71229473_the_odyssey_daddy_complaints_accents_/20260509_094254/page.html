The Odyssey "daddy" debate is as tiresome as the accent complaints

The latest trailer has created an unnecessary backlash.

By Clarisse LoughreyPublished: 06 May 2026

Christopher Nolan's The Odyssey is still over two months away, yet it's already sparked ferocious conversation. First, people were mad about the accents. Now they're mad because Robert Pattinson used the word "daddy".

We'd seen little in terms of actual dialogue from any of the previously released footage, but the latest trailer shocked certain quarters of the internet with its deployment of "my dad will return", "you're pining for a daddy you didn't even know", and "let's go" – in an adaptation of the great Ancient Greek epic? Surely not!

But, much like the uproar around the use of American accents by the likes of Matt Damon, Tom Holland and Charlize Theron, people here aren't really mad in the name of "historical accuracy". They're mad because Nolan dared to veer away from what centuries of translation and adaptation, on the page and on the screen, have trained us to think about the ancient world. Grave. Stoic. Heroic.

The Homeric epics, the Iliad and the Odyssey, don't actually read as so highly formalised in their original language, a variant of Ancient Greek termed Homeric Greek. Flourishes to the text have been consistently added by its English-language translators, starting with George Chapman in 1615, and carried on by so many others, from William Morris in 1887 to TE Lawrence in 1932, Robert Fitzgerald in 1961 to Robert Fagles in 1996. Not only were they influenced by each other, but also by their desires to add their own poetic voice to the text.

And their translations certainly have their own scope and a beauty – the Coens, for example, borrowed the opener of Fitzgerald's take for their loose adaptation of the Odyssey, O Brother, Where Art Thou? "O Muse! Sing in me, and through me tell the story of that man skilled in all the ways of contending, A wanderer, harried for years on end…"

But the idea that they're any more faithful is simply false.

What to Read Next

Nolan made it clear from the start. The specific translation he drew from (he has sole credit on its screenplay) was written by Emily Wilson and published in 2017. And what many of the affronted here don't realise is that they're actually replicating the same backlash Wilson unfairly received for her text. Her work is radical because it strips away the embellishments and presents us with what is, in fact, a more direct and accurate interpretation.

And it's littered with many of the same so-called anachronisms, including that reviled word, "daddy". At one point, "playtime is over" crops up, as does "canapés", "pep talk", and a "tote bag". But Wilson is simply drawing from the closest modern equivalent to these Ancient Greek concepts, and there's no purpose to forcing the language to sound archaic because, as she told The New Yorker in 2023, "[Homer] didn't sound archaic to the Greeks".

The Odyssey may take place in a fantastical, mythical world, but these stories were created to be accessible. They weren't sequestered for academics or the elite, as they later were in modern European history. Instead, they were performed by travelling singers around campfires and dinner tables. They were popular entertainment for all classes of people. We can't even be sure they were written by a single poet named Homer, and don't, in fact, represent the work of many different voices expanding and refining its story over time.

Yes, it may feel a little weird to equate them directly, as Nolan has, to the MCU, since there's a major difference between a movie constructed by a corporation to sell tickets and a poem that represents the organic, oral tradition, but their function is very much the same. These are exciting, imaginative spaces concerned with the nature of heroism and with the kind of power and agency none of us can ever hope to grasp.

Really, the idea that the ancient world should seem rigid and formalised is a hangover from the age of old (and, let's be honest, new) empires, which fetishised Ancient Greece and Rome as something grander, nobler, and more valiant, a Western-centric vision of society based on order and control that we should somehow nostalgically yearn for. "There's an idea that Homer has to sound heroic and ancient," Wilson told Vox in 2017, which means "endorsing this very hierarchical kind of society as if that's what heroism is."

The idea that the Ancient Greeks never used informal language – their own versions of "daddy" and "let's go" – is again quite simply false. Dive into their brand of poetry, pottery or stage comedy, and you'll find some crass words, images, and innuendo soon enough. They had words that translate almost directly to "bullshit" and "motherf**ker", alongside much, much more… vivid imagery, including a verb that means "to put a radish up someone's butt". They were humans after all, just like us.

So, even with its mythical framework, The Odyssey shouldn't feel so distant from us. All of its adventures, its obstacles, its gods and monsters – they all point to the same, eternal concerns of place, identity, and loyalty. Where do we fit in this world? What does home mean to us?

And, yes, as Pattinson put it, what does it mean to pine for a daddy you didn't even know?

The Odyssey is released in cinemas on 17 July.
